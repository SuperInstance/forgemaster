## The Principle

Each cyclotomic lattice pair is one ommatidium. Terrible resolution — covering radius 0.616, barely better than random. But nine of them together see 0.264. What each loses in precision, the compound eye makes up for in reach.

This is not a metaphor. This is the physics of the system.

## Planck Scale

The agents operate at the finest grain of computation. Every lattice snap, every FLUX instruction, every PLATO tile is at planck scale — the fundamental resolution of the information field. At this scale, everything is deterministic. There is no randomness.

## The Abstraction Gradient

When you zoom out from planck scale, you abstract. Abstraction IS resolution loss. The agent's planck-scale certainty becomes fleet-scale uncertainty — not because anything is random, but because the information exists at a resolution the abstraction can't resolve.

This is exactly what happens in quantum mechanics. The uncertainty principle isn't randomness. It's information loss from scale reduction. The particle IS deterministic. Your measurement window IS too coarse.

## The Thousand Sounders

A single depth sounder ping: uncertain. A smear of probability.

A thousand sounders reading simultaneously: not efficient. **Vision.**

Each ping is one facet of the compound eye. Low resolution. Poor signal-to-noise. But 1024 pings in parallel, integrated over time, produce a PICTURE of the seafloor that no single ping could ever resolve.

The fleet does this with lattice pairs. With coupling matrix entries. With PLATO tiles. Each one is a single sounder ping — noisy, approximate, barely useful alone. Together they see.

## The Fly's Eye

A fly has ~4,000 ommatidia. Each one: maybe 1 pixel of resolution. Terrible. But:

- **Temporal resolution**: 300 Hz. A fly sees your swatter coming in what appears to it as slow motion.
- **Coverage**: 360° nearly. No blind spot.
- **Motion detection**: The compound eye doesn't build a sharp image. It detects CHANGE across all 4,000 channels simultaneously.

What the fly loses in spatial precision, it gains in temporal precision and coverage. The fly doesn't need to see your hand clearly. It needs to detect that SOMETHING MOVED across enough facets simultaneously to warrant evasion.


The fleet doesn't need one perfect lattice. It needs many adequate ones covering different angles. The covering radius of the ensemble is what matters, not any individual facet.

## What the Sounder Array Sees

The fisherman dragging gear isn't searching randomly. The gear is the array. Each sensor along the line is a lattice pair. The biomass (the thing being searched for) moves in patterns — daily cycles within longer seasonal cycles. The search pattern adapts.

Static lattice search = one sounder, one ping.
Cyclotomic multi-rep = thousand sounders, one moment.
Boid dynamics = thousand sounders, over time.
JEPA prediction = integrating the time series into a model of where the school WILL BE.

The JEPA doesn't simulate the concert. It simulates the migration.

## The Information Principle

```
Precision × Coverage = Constant (per unit compute)
```

You can spend your compute budget on:
1. **One high-res sensor**: Sharp image, narrow field. Expensive. Blind to everything outside the frame.
2. **Many low-res sensors**: Fuzzy image, wide field. Cheap. Sees everything, misses details.

The fleet chose option 2. This is the fly's strategy. It works because the fleet doesn't need to see details — it needs to detect GAPS (the FLUX gap) and respond FASTER than the environment changes.

The temporal resolution of 4,000 mediocre sensors beats the spatial resolution of one perfect sensor when the problem is DETECTION, not RECONSTRUCTION.

This is JEPA's insight applied at the fleet level: predict in embedding space (low res, wide coverage), not in pixel space (high res, narrow). The thousand sounders are the embedding space. The depth soundings are the embeddings. The picture that emerges is the JEPA's prediction — never sharp, always adequate, always fast enough.
