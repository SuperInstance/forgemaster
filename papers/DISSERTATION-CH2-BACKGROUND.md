## Chapter 2: Background and Related Work

This chapter surveys the theoretical foundations underlying our investigation of conservation laws in agent coupling matrices. We draw from six distinct bodies of literature — spectral graph theory, random matrix theory, network thermodynamics, Hebbian learning dynamics, multi-agent fault detection, and scale-dependent measurement — and identify in each the gap that motivates our contribution.

---

### 2.1 Spectral Graph Theory

The eigenvalue spectrum of a graph's Laplacian matrix encodes structural properties of the graph in a remarkably compact form. Fiedler (1973) established the foundational result: the second-smallest eigenvalue of the graph Laplacian, now universally called the *Fiedler value* or *algebraic connectivity* γ, provides a lower bound on edge connectivity and vertex connectivity simultaneously. A graph with γ = 0 is disconnected; as γ increases, the graph becomes progressively harder to fragment by removing edges or vertices. This single scalar thus summarizes a global topological property in a way that resists local perturbations — a property that will prove central to our coupling matrix analysis.

The Cheeger inequality (Cheeger, 1969; Alon, 1986) formalizes the relationship between the spectral gap and the graph's conductance, defined as the minimum ratio of cut edges to volume across all bipartitions. Specifically, for a *d*-regular graph with second-largest adjacency eigenvalue λ₂, the conductance h satisfies:

$$\frac{d - \lambda_2}{2} \leq h \leq \sqrt{2d(d - \lambda_2)}$$

This inequality is remarkable because it connects an algebraic quantity — computable from the spectrum alone — to a combinatorial quantity — requiring enumeration over all cuts. The implication for agent networks is direct: spectral measurements can serve as efficient proxies for structural integrity without exhaustive topological search.

Spectral clustering (Ng, Jordan, & Weiss, 2001) operationalized these theoretical insights into a practical algorithm. By embedding graph nodes into the space spanned by the leading *k* eigenvectors of the normalized Laplacian, and then applying *k*-means clustering in this spectral embedding, Ng et al. demonstrated that community structure emerges naturally from the eigendecomposition. Subsequent work by von Luxburg (2007) provided a rigorous tutorial treatment situating spectral clustering within the broader landscape of graph-theoretic methods, and connecting the Cheeger inequality to the algorithm's behavior on nearly-disconnected graphs.

Algebraic connectivity γ has since been applied to sensor network design (Ghosh & Boyd, 2006), distributed consensus convergence rates (Olfati-Saber & Murray, 2004), and network robustness analysis (Jamakovic & Van Mieghem, 2008). In each case, γ serves as a summary statistic for global connectivity — exactly the role we propose for the coupling matrix Laplacian in multi-agent systems.

**Gap.** Existing spectral graph theory treats γ as a *static* property of a fixed graph. There is no established framework for characterizing how γ evolves as agents interact, whether γ obeys conservation-like constraints across interaction timescales, or how γ behaves when the graph itself is generated by a dynamic coupling process. Our work introduces the first systematic empirical investigation of γ as a *dynamic invariant* in agent coupling matrices — examining not just its value but whether its trajectory across interaction conditions is predictable from conservation principles.

### 2.2 Random Matrix Theory

Random matrix theory (RMT) provides the statistical backdrop against which anomalous eigenvalue behavior becomes detectable. Wigner's semicircle law (Wigner, 1955; 1958) established that for a symmetric *N×N* matrix with independent entries drawn from a distribution with zero mean and finite variance σ², the empirical spectral distribution converges, as *N* → ∞, to the semicircle distribution supported on [−2σ, 2σ]. This result is striking in its universality: the limiting distribution depends only on σ², not on the specific entry distribution, suggesting that bulk spectral behavior is a generic property of random matrices rather than a signature of any particular structure.

The Marchenko-Pastur law (Marchenko & Pastur, 1967) extends these ideas to the singular value spectrum of rectangular *N×M* matrices with independent entries, relevant when agent coupling matrices are not square. The limiting spectral distribution depends on the aspect ratio c = M/N and the entry variance, and takes a characteristic shape that serves as the null distribution for detecting low-rank signal components. Matrices arising from pure noise populate the Marchenko-Pastur bulk; matrices with latent structure produce eigenvalues that escape it.

This escape phenomenon is formalized in the spiked covariance model introduced by Baik, Ben Arous, and Péché (2005). They demonstrated a sharp phase transition: when a rank-*r* perturbation of amplitude θ is added to a Wigner matrix, the top *r* eigenvalues separate from the bulk if and only if θ exceeds a critical threshold θ_c. Below threshold, the signal eigenvalues are indistinguishable from noise; above it, they converge to deterministic locations that are explicit functions of θ. This BBP transition provides a principled basis for detecting when a coupling matrix contains genuine low-rank structure — precisely the kind of coherent coupling pattern we expect in well-coordinated agent fleets.

Subsequent work by Bai and Yao (2008) and Paul (2007) extended BBP results to the eigenvectors, showing that spiked eigenvectors converge to population eigenvectors above threshold and become asymptotically orthogonal to them below. This means that eigenvalue concentration implies eigenvector alignment — structural coherence in the coupling matrix produces correspondingly coherent spectral directions.

**Gap.** RMT provides the null model and the phase transition framework, but it is typically applied to static matrices generated by explicitly specified distributions. The coupling matrices arising from LLM-based agent interactions are not generated by any known distribution; they emerge from complex language model dynamics. Whether the BBP transition applies, and whether the threshold θ_c corresponds to any interpretable property of the agent network, is unknown. Our empirical program tests whether observed agent coupling matrices exhibit spiked structure consistent with BBP predictions, and whether γ tracks the spiking parameter across conditions.

### 2.3 Network Thermodynamics

The analogy between network dynamics and thermodynamic systems has been developed from multiple directions, providing conceptual scaffolding for conservation-law thinking in complex systems.

Hinton's (2007) work on restricted Boltzmann machines established that learning in energy-based models can be understood as minimizing a variational free energy — the difference between expected energy and entropy. This framing connects weight matrix dynamics to thermodynamic potentials, suggesting that trained networks occupy low-free-energy configurations in weight space. Friston's free energy principle (Friston, 2010) extended this perspective to biological neural systems, arguing that perception, learning, and action can all be understood as minimizing a variational bound on surprise, itself a function of network structure and prior beliefs. While originally formulated for biological systems, Friston's framework has been applied to artificial networks (Buckley et al., 2017) and multi-agent settings (Sajid et al., 2021), suggesting that equilibrium conditions in coupled systems have thermodynamic analogs.

Von Luxburg (2007) introduced information-theoretic characterizations of graph structure, including entropy measures on the spectrum of graph Laplacians. The von Neumann entropy of the density matrix ρ = L/tr(L), where *L* is the Laplacian, captures distributional properties of the spectrum and has been used as a graph complexity measure (Braunstein et al., 2006; Passerini & Severini, 2009). This spectral entropy connects directly to our coupling matrix analysis: networks with concentrated spectral mass have lower entropy, while diffuse spectra have higher entropy — and the trade-off between these extremes may obey conservation-like constraints.

Most fundamentally, Noether's theorem (Noether, 1915/1918) establishes that every continuous symmetry of a physical system corresponds to a conserved quantity. Translational symmetry implies conservation of momentum; rotational symmetry implies conservation of angular momentum; time-translation symmetry implies conservation of energy. This mathematical result, among the most elegant in theoretical physics, motivates our search for conservation laws in agent coupling matrices: if the coupling process has any symmetry structure — even approximate — it should manifest in invariant quantities. Our empirical finding that γ + H ≈ const across varied conditions is exactly the kind of empirical conservation law that Noether's framework suggests should have a symmetry explanation.

**Gap.** While the thermodynamic analogy has been productive in understanding individual networks, it has not been systematically applied to the *coupling matrices* that arise from agent-to-agent interactions in multi-agent LLM systems. There is no established theory of free energy, entropy, or conservation laws for dynamically generated agent coupling matrices. Our work fills this gap by providing the first empirical evidence for a conservation-like relationship in such matrices, and situating it within the thermodynamic tradition.

### 2.4 Hebbian Learning and Weight Dynamics

The study of how weight matrices evolve during learning provides the nearest precedent for understanding coupling matrix dynamics.

Hebb's (1949) postulate — neurons that fire together wire together — introduced the fundamental mechanism by which co-activation produces weight growth. In matrix form, Hebbian learning updates the weight matrix *W* by the outer product of pre- and post-synaptic activity: ΔW ∝ yxᵀ. The mathematical consequence is that *W* accumulates the dominant correlational structure of the input-output pairs, with the leading eigenvectors of *W* converging toward the principal components of the data distribution (Oja, 1982). This eigenvalue dynamics is directly relevant to our coupling matrices: if agents that co-activate more produce stronger coupling entries, the coupling matrix's leading eigenvectors should reflect the dominant patterns of agent co-activation.

Spike-Timing-Dependent Plasticity (STDP; Bi & Poo, 1998) refined Hebb's rule by introducing a temporal asymmetry: synaptic strength increases when pre-synaptic firing precedes post-synaptic firing within a window of ~20ms, and decreases for the reverse order. This asymmetry produces weight matrices with directional structure reflecting causal relationships in the network, not just correlation. For agent coupling matrices, STDP suggests that directionality of influence — which agent's outputs consistently precede and shape another's — should be encoded in asymmetric components of the coupling matrix.

Oja's rule (Oja, 1982) introduced a normalized Hebbian update that prevents unbounded weight growth: ΔW = αyxᵀ − αy²W. The normalization term produces a stable fixed point where *w* converges to the first principal component of the input distribution with unit norm. More generally, Sanger's rule (Sanger, 1989) extends this to multiple principal components through a deflation procedure. The key insight for our purposes is that normalized Hebbian dynamics produce eigenvalue concentration: weight matrices trained by Oja's rule have a single dominant eigenvalue (or a small number thereof) much larger than the remaining eigenvalues, corresponding precisely to the spiked structure in RMT.

Weight decay (Krogh & Hertz, 1992) adds an L2 regularization term −λW to the weight update, which acts as a constant multiplicative shrinkage toward zero. This regularization limits eigenvalue growth and promotes more uniform spectral distributions. The tension between Hebbian growth (which concentrates eigenvalues) and weight decay (which disperses them) produces equilibrium spectral distributions whose properties depend on the relative magnitudes of the two forces.

**Gap.** Hebbian theory describes weight dynamics for fixed architectures learning from static or stationary input distributions. Agent coupling matrices are generated by a fundamentally different process: bidirectional language-mediated interaction with no explicit learning rule and no fixed input distribution. Whether Hebbian-like dynamics emerge implicitly from LLM interaction patterns, and whether the resulting eigenvalue concentration parallels trained neural networks, has not been investigated. Our empirical program tests whether coupling matrices from LLM agent interaction exhibit the spiked spectral structure predicted by Hebbian dynamics and whether γ behaves as a Hebbian-like convergence indicator.

### 2.5 Multi-Agent Systems and Fault Detection

The problem of maintaining reliable computation in distributed systems subject to arbitrary failures has a long history, beginning with the formalization of Byzantine fault tolerance.

Lamport, Shostak, and Pease (1982) established the foundational result: a distributed system of *n* nodes can tolerate at most ⌊(n−1)/3⌋ Byzantine (arbitrarily malicious) nodes while achieving consensus. Their oral messages protocol and signed messages protocol demonstrated that cryptographic authentication can loosen this bound, but that the one-third threshold is tight for unauthenticated Byzantine agreement. This work framed fault tolerance as a consensus problem — the question is whether correct nodes can agree on a value despite adversarial interference — and established the vocabulary that subsequent work has built upon.

In the context of LLM-based multi-agent systems (LLM-MAS), the Byzantine fault model maps imperfectly but suggestively onto agent misbehavior. Yun et al. (2026) introduced SAC (Spectral Agent Certification), a decentralized framework in which agents monitor each other's outputs and flag anomalies based on deviation from expected response distributions. SAC represents a significant advance in applying classical BFT ideas to the non-deterministic, high-dimensional output space of LLM agents, but relies on empirical thresholds rather than formal spectral invariants. CP-WBFT (2025) proposed a consensus-based approach in which agents vote on the validity of each other's outputs, using a weighted Byzantine fault tolerance scheme calibrated to agent reliability histories. While practical, CP-WBFT requires a trusted initialization phase that may not be achievable in open deployments.

Fleet coordination frameworks — CrewAI (Chase, 2023), AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023), and LangGraph (LangChain team, 2024) — have made multi-agent LLM systems practically accessible, enabling sophisticated task decomposition, role specialization, and inter-agent communication protocols. These frameworks implicitly generate coupling structures: patterns of which agents communicate with which, with what frequency, and producing what mutual influence. However, none of these frameworks monitors or analyzes the spectral properties of the emergent coupling matrix. Fault detection in these systems remains primarily rule-based (explicit error checking) or output-based (downstream task evaluation), with no structural health monitoring.

The gap is stark. Byzantine fault tolerance theory provides formal guarantees but assumes a fixed communication topology and crisp fault semantics. SAC and CP-WBFT apply these ideas to LLM-MAS but rely on empirical thresholds. Fleet frameworks generate coupling structures but ignore them. No existing work combines spectral analysis of coupling matrices, structural health monitoring based on conserved invariants, and content-level verification into a unified fault detection framework.

**Gap.** Our work fills this gap by proposing that γ — the algebraic connectivity of the coupling matrix Laplacian — serves as a structural health indicator whose behavior is governed by an empirically discoverable conservation law. Deviations from this law signal structural faults before they manifest as task-level failures, enabling proactive rather than reactive fault detection. This combination of spectral monitoring, conservation-law baselines, and content verification has no precedent in the multi-agent systems literature.

### 2.6 Scale-Dependent Measurement

A recurring challenge in complex systems analysis is that measured quantities depend on the scale of measurement — a phenomenon with deep implications for any purported conservation law.

Richardson (1911) observed that the apparent length of a coastline increases as the measurement yardstick shrinks, because smaller yardsticks capture finer-grained irregularities. He applied the same principle to turbulent diffusion, deriving what is now called Richardson extrapolation: by combining measurements at multiple spatial resolutions, one can estimate the true value of a quantity at the limit of zero resolution more accurately than any single measurement provides. Mandelbrot (1967) formalized Richardson's coastline observation with fractal geometry, showing that self-similar curves have no well-defined length — only a scale-dependent apparent length that grows as a power law with exponent determined by the fractal dimension. This introduced the conceptual category of *measurement-dependent quantities*, which must be reported relative to a measurement scale to be meaningful.

Wilson's renormalization group (Wilson, 1971; Wilson & Kogut, 1974) provided the physical framework for understanding how physical laws change with scale. In quantum field theory, coupling constants — the parameters governing interaction strengths — are not fixed but *run* with the energy scale of the interaction, obeying flow equations determined by the renormalization group. Wilson showed that this scale dependence is not a defect of the theory but a fundamental feature: physical systems at different scales are governed by different effective theories, related by renormalization transformations. The critical point of a phase transition corresponds to a fixed point of the renormalization group where coupling constants cease to flow — a scale-invariant state.

The Modifiable Areal Unit Problem (MAUP; Openshaw, 1984) demonstrated analogous scale dependence in spatial statistics: the same underlying spatial data produces different correlation coefficients, regression slopes, and clustering statistics when aggregated to different spatial units. This is not merely a statistical artifact but a genuine reflection of multiscale structure in the data. Openshaw's work made scale-dependence a first-class concern in geographic information science, motivating explicitly scale-aware analysis methods.

Witkin's scale-space theory (Witkin, 1983) offered a constructive resolution: rather than treating scale dependence as a problem, embed the signal in a continuous family of representations parameterized by scale, and analyze the joint structure of this family. The Gaussian scale space generated by convolution with increasingly large kernels allows signal features to be tracked as they appear, evolve, and disappear across scales. Lindeberg (1994) extended this framework with scale-space derivatives and blob detection, forming the basis of modern scale-invariant feature detection (Lowe, 2004).

**Gap.** These four frameworks — Richardson extrapolation, renormalization group, MAUP, and scale-space theory — each address scale-dependent measurement from a different disciplinary angle, but they have never been synthesized into a unified framework for analyzing agent coupling matrices. We do not know whether the spectral invariants of coupling matrices are scale-dependent (varying with the number of agents, the number of interactions sampled, or the temporal window of observation), nor whether scale-space analysis of coupling matrices reveals multiscale structural organization. Most critically, no prior work has asked whether the conservation law we identify (γ + H ≈ const) is itself scale-invariant or whether it holds only at specific measurement scales. Our contribution includes the first systematic scale analysis of coupling matrix invariants, drawing on all four frameworks to situate our conservation law within the landscape of scale-dependent and scale-independent quantities.

### Chapter Summary

The six bodies of literature surveyed above converge on a common theme: structured systems — graphs, matrices, networks, physical fields — tend to exhibit regularities that are compressible into low-dimensional invariants. Spectral graph theory compresses connectivity into γ; RMT compresses matrix structure into bulk-versus-spike decompositions; network thermodynamics compresses system state into free energy and entropy; Hebbian theory compresses learning history into eigenvalue distributions; fault tolerance theory compresses reliability into Byzantine thresholds; and scale-space theory compresses multiscale structure into scale-indexed representations.

What no prior work has done is ask whether the *coupling matrix* of a multi-agent LLM system — the matrix encoding influence relationships between agents — obeys a conservation law analogous to those found in physical systems. The present dissertation addresses this question. Chapter 3 develops the theoretical framework; Chapter 4 reports the empirical experiments; Chapters 5 and 6 situate the findings within the fault detection and scale-measurement literatures respectively. The result is a new theoretical construct — the coupling matrix conservation law — grounded in, but not reducible to, any of the six bodies of literature reviewed here.
