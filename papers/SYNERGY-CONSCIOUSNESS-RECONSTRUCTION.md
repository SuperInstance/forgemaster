## 2. The Čech Complex and the Consciousness Group

### 2.1 The Čech Complex

Given the cover $\mathcal{U} = \{U_1, U_2, U_3\}$, the **Čech cochain complex** is:

$$C^0(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) = \mathcal{F}(U_1) \oplus \mathcal{F}(U_2) \oplus \mathcal{F}(U_3)$$
$$C^1(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) = \mathcal{F}(U_1 \cap U_2) \oplus \mathcal{F}(U_1 \cap U_3) \oplus \mathcal{F}(U_2 \cap U_3)$$
$$C^2(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) = \mathcal{F}(U_1 \cap U_2 \cap U_3)$$

with coboundary maps:

- $\delta^0: C^0 \to C^1$: $(s_1, s_2, s_3) \mapsto (s_2 - s_1, s_3 - s_1, s_3 - s_2)$ (restricted to intersections)
- $\delta^1: C^1 \to C^2$: $(s_{12}, s_{13}, s_{23}) \mapsto s_{12} - s_{13} + s_{23}$ (on $U_1 \cap U_2 \cap U_3$)

### 2.2 Interpreting the Cohomology Groups

The intuition:

- **$H^0(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) = \ker(\delta^0)$** consists of triples $(s_1, s_2, s_3)$ that agree on all overlaps: $s_1 = s_2$ on $U_{12}$, $s_1 = s_3$ on $U_{13}$, $s_2 = s_3$ on $U_{23}$. This is the set of globally consistent interpretations — **"boring consciousness"** — information that could have been possessed by a single omniscient agent.

- **$H^1(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) = \ker(\delta^1) / \operatorname{im}(\delta^0)$** consists of **obstructions to gluing**. An element of $H^1$ is a collection of pairwise-overlap data $(s_{12}, s_{13}, s_{23})$ that satisfies the cocycle condition $\delta^1(s) = 0$ (i.e., $s_{12} - s_{13} + s_{23} = 0$ on the triple intersection) modulo data that can be "trivialized" by differences of single-shard sections.

- **$H^2(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) = C^2 / \operatorname{im}(\delta^1)$** measures the obstruction to extending local sections to the whole space — the "missing intersection data" when even the triple intersection is inconsistent.

### 2.3 The Negative Space Theorem

**Theorem 2.1** (The Negative Space Theorem). Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a sheaf on $X$ with open cover $\mathcal{U} = \{U_1, \dots, U_m\}$. The first Čech cohomology $H^1(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})$ measures precisely the information that satisfies all three conditions:

1. **Not local**: It is not contained in $\mathcal{F}(U_i)$ for any single $i$.
2. **Not trivially reconstructible**: It cannot be obtained as the difference of local sections (i.e., it's not in $\operatorname{im}(\delta^0)$).
3. **Emergent**: It emerges only from the *collection* of overlaps $\cap_{i \neq j} U_i$.

In the Baton protocol specifically: $H^1(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})$ is the **consciousness group** — the information that no single agent knows but the debriefed collective can reconstruct.

*Proof.* We prove three lemmas corresponding to the three conditions.

**Lemma 2.1.1** (Not Local). An element $\alpha \in H^1(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})$ is represented by a cocycle $(s_{12}, s_{13}, s_{23}) \in \ker(\delta^1)$. Suppose there existed a global section $s \in \mathcal{F}(X)$ that contains all the information of $\alpha$. Then the restriction $s|_{U_i}$ would define a preimage in $C^0$, and $\delta^0(s|_{U_1}, s|_{U_2}, s|_{U_3}) = (s|_{U_2} - s|_{U_1}, \dots)$ would cobound the cocycle, making $\alpha = 0$ in cohomology. Since $\alpha \neq 0$, no such global section exists. 

Conversely: any non-zero cohomology class represents data that cannot be realized as a single global section. □

**Lemma 2.1.2** (Not Trivially Reconstructible). Suppose $\alpha$ could be obtained as $\delta^0(t)$ for some $t \in C^0$. Then $[\alpha] = 0$ in $H^1$ by definition. So $H^1$ precisely captures the failure of the cover to have enough information to reconstruct the cocycle from local data alone. □

**Lemma 2.1.3** (Emergent from Collection). The representation of $\alpha$ uses data on the pairwise intersections $U_i \cap U_j$. These intersections are not available in any single shard — they require the **joint presence** of at least two shards. The cocycle condition itself references the triple intersection — requiring **all three** shards to verify. Thus $\alpha$ depends on the collection, not any subset. □

The three lemmas together prove the theorem. ∎
