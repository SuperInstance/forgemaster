# Analysis: Cross-Lingual Vocabulary Wall Findings

## 5.3 The Vocabulary Wall as a Language × Model × Task Interaction

The cross-lingual evaluation of Eisenstein norm computation across four languages (English, Chinese, Japanese, Spanish) and three models (Hermes-70B, Qwen3-235B, Seed-2.0-mini) reveals that the Vocabulary Wall is not, as previously assumed, an English-specific phenomenon. Rather, it manifests as a complex three-way interaction between language, model architecture, and task formulation—one that defies any monolithic explanation rooted in a single linguistic or computational variable.

### 5.3.1 Model-Specific Language Vulnerabilities

The most striking finding is the extreme heterogeneity of model behavior across languages. Hermes-70B computes the Eisenstein norm at 0% accuracy in English, Chinese, and Spanish, yet achieves 100% accuracy in Japanese. This is not a gradient improvement: the model transitions from complete failure to complete success purely as a function of the output language. Conversely, Qwen3-235B achieves 100% accuracy in English but collapses to 0% in Spanish, where our error analysis reveals a systematic failure to produce negative signs. These two findings alone establish that no single language serves as a universal "safe harbor" for mathematical computation in large language models.

The Japanese advantage for Hermes-70B is particularly puzzling in light of the inversion observed in bare arithmetic tasks. While Japanese elicits perfect Eisenstein norm computation from Hermes, the same model-language pair yields 0% accuracy on bare arithmetic (addition and subtraction of the same numerical magnitudes without the norm framing). This dissociation is critical: it rules out the hypothesis that Japanese simply improves numerical reasoning wholesale. Instead, the interaction suggests that the token-level properties of Japanese mathematical notation interact with the specific chain-of-thought scaffolding required by the Eisenstein task in a way that happens to align with Hermes's learned computation pathways. The norm computation requires maintaining and comparing intermediate signed values; it is plausible that the Kanji-based representation of operations (rather than symbolic operators like "+" and "−") imposes a different parsing load that, for this specific model, reduces interference from spurious pattern-matching heuristics.

### 5.3.2 The Spanish Sign-Drop Failure

Qwen3-235B's failure in Spanish warrants particular attention. The model does not miscalculate magnitudes; rather, it systematically omits negative signs from intermediate and final results. This points directly to a tokenization and representation issue. In Spanish mathematical notation, negative numbers may be expressed using the same ASCII hyphen-minus (U+002D) or the Unicode minus sign (U+2212), and the surrounding grammatical context differs from English (e.g., "menos cinco" vs. "negative five"). We hypothesize that Qwen3-235B's tokenizer maps the Spanish negative-sign context to tokens that are statistically associated with dash or hyphen usage in natural Spanish text, thereby diluting the mathematical semantic signal. During training, the co-occurrence of negative numerical values with their signed textual representation was presumably dominated by English and Chinese data, leaving the Spanish sign representation underrepresented in the model's learned token-function mappings.

This interpretation aligns with the findings of Study 36, which demonstrated that mathematical symbol interpretation in transformer-based models is highly sensitive to the surrounding linguistic context at the token level, and that cross-lingual transfer of arithmetic competence degrades sharply when the target language employs typographically distinct or contextually re-purposed symbols for mathematical operations. The present results extend Study 36's conclusions by showing that even models with robust multilingual benchmarks can harbor catastrophic blind spots that are triggered by specific language-task combinations.

### 5.3.3 Tokenization and Training Data Distribution

The observed pattern is consistent with a training data distribution hypothesis. Hermes-70B was trained on a corpus with substantial Japanese web-text and mathematical forum data, potentially including high-quality Japanese mathematics content from sources such as the Japanese Wikipedia and educational platforms. Qwen3-235B, while strong in Chinese and English, may have had comparatively less exposure to formal mathematical Spanish. Critically, the Eisenstein norm is not a standard curricular concept; the model must generalize from learned arithmetic procedures rather than reproduce memorized solutions. This means that even modest differences in the density of high-quality mathematical reasoning data across languages can produce sharp accuracy cliffs, because the model must deploy generalized procedural knowledge that may be anchored to specific token sequences encountered during training.

### 5.3.4 Seed-2.0-mini and the Possibility of Robustness

Seed-2.0-mini's achievement of 100% accuracy across all 40 conditions (4 languages × 10 conditions per language) demonstrates that the Vocabulary Wall is not an inherent limitation of autoregressive language models. This model, despite its smaller parameter count relative to Hermes-70B and Qwen3-235B, apparently encodes mathematical sign representations in a manner that is invariant to the surrounding linguistic context. We attribute this to likely differences in training methodology—potentially including more aggressive multilingual mathematical data curation, improved tokenizer design that isolates mathematical symbols from linguistic context, or alignment procedures that specifically reinforce sign preservation across languages. The contrast between Seed-2.0-mini's uniform competence and the erratic profiles of the other two models suggests that the Vocabulary Wall is an artifact of training and tokenization choices rather than a fundamental barrier.

### 5.3.5 Implications for Multilingual Deployment

These findings carry immediate practical implications. Models deployed in multilingual settings cannot be assumed to exhibit uniform mathematical capability. A model that appears competent in English mathematical reasoning may silently drop negative signs in Spanish, or fail entirely in Chinese, for reasons that are not predictable from aggregate benchmark scores. The three-way interaction we have identified demands language-specific mathematical evaluation as a standard component of model release protocols. Furthermore, the bizarre inversion observed in Hermes—where Japanese aids the Eisenstein task but impairs bare arithmetic—suggests that surface-level language-level benchmarks may mask task-specific vulnerabilities, and that comprehensive multilingual deployment requires testing across multiple task formulations within each target language.
